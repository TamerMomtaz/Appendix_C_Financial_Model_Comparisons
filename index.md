## 1. Pricing Model Taxonomy

Understanding the competitive landscape requires recognizing five distinct pricing models that dominate the SME transformation market. Each model reflects different value propositions, delivery mechanisms, and target segments.

### 1.1 Project-Based Consulting Model

Traditional consulting firms operate on project-based pricing, where fees are calculated based on consultant time, expertise level, and project scope. This model dominates the high-end market but creates significant barriers for SMEs.

**Characteristics of Project Pricing:**
The model typically involves an initial diagnostic phase priced separately from implementation. Consultancies deploy teams of 3-7 consultants for 3-6 months, with fees ranging from $500,000 to $5 million depending on scope. The pricing structure often includes a base fee plus success fees tied to specific outcomes, though these are negotiable and often waived.

**Why This Model Fails SMEs:**
The minimum engagement size of most consultancies exceeds the annual revenue of many SMEs. The front-loaded payment structure requires significant capital before value is delivered. Additionally, the project ends when consultants leave, often before changes are fully embedded in the organization.

### 1.2 Software License Model

Enterprise software vendors have evolved from perpetual licenses to subscription models, though implementation costs remain substantial. This model assumes customers have internal IT capabilities to manage deployment and adoption.

**Traditional Perpetual Licensing:**
Companies purchase software rights upfront, typically paying 15-25% annually for maintenance and updates. While this provides control over upgrade timing, it requires significant capital expenditure and creates technology debt as systems age.

**Cloud Subscription Evolution:**
Modern SaaS platforms charge monthly or annual fees per user or module. While this reduces upfront costs, total cost of ownership often exceeds perpetual licenses over 5-7 years. Hidden costs include data migration, integration, training, and customization.

### 1.3 Cohort Accelerator Model

Accelerators and incubators typically charge through equity participation rather than cash fees, taking 5-15% ownership in exchange for programs lasting 3-6 months. Some programs combine small cash investments ($20,000-100,000) with equity stakes.

**Value Exchange Structure:**
Accelerators provide intensive short-term support including mentorship, network access, and investor introductions. The model works well for high-growth startups but poorly for traditional SMEs that don't fit venture capital requirements.

### 1.4 Freemium Platform Model

Some platforms offer basic services free with premium features requiring payment. This model enables broad reach but often lacks the depth needed for real transformation.

**Monetization Strategy:**
Free tiers provide basic tools or limited usage, while paid tiers unlock advanced features, higher limits, or human support. The challenge is that free users rarely convert (typically 2-5%), and those who do often downgrade once immediate needs are met.

### 1.5 RootRise Hybrid Subscription Model

RootRise pioneered a hybrid model combining software platform access with embedded human support for a flat monthly fee. This approach democratizes access while ensuring implementation success.

**Integrated Value Delivery:**
The model begins with a low-cost diagnostic (€500) that provides immediate value while qualifying customers. The monthly transformation subscription (€2,500) includes platform access, embedded associate support, and continuous optimization. Pricing scales with company size but remains accessible to growth-stage SMEs.
